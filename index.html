2008: The Decision

A market-based decision that served rural NC and conservative principles.

The 2008 abatement reflected a principle most NC legislators on both sides of this debate share: government should set things up so private markets can work, rather than replace them entirely. Solar properties pay taxes on just 20% of assessed value; no state spending, no mandates, just a stable signal. The result: an estimated $59.1 billion in clean energy economic impact from 2007–2022 (Petrusa et al., 2023). Even for people who don’t support solar energy, the way this policy is set up is fairly conservative.

Solar developers pay taxes on only 20% of the assessed property value, and the state does not directly spend money to support this structure.

Every one of these counties received over $500M in solar investment (Petrusa et al., 2023):

Duplin Robeson Halifax Edgecombe Northampton Bladen

The concern that solar development threatens NC farmland is understandable and are worth examining. The data itself shows that solar and agriculture are not necessarily in conflict. Pandey et al. (2025) found that agrivoltaic systems, where solar and active farming share the same land, can actually improve agricultural outcomes. This shows that solar and farmland protections can be compatible goals if implemented correctly.

The shared interest: Both supporters and opponents of HB 729 want rural NC communities to thrive. The question is whether the bill, as written, advances that goal or whether it undermines the fiscal stability those communities have built over 17 years while not meaningfully protecting the farmland its title claims to defend.

What Solar Means for Landowners

For the farmer who leases, solar is the best crop they've ever grown.

The economic case for solar doesn't just live in county budgets, it alsolives on individual farms. Carey & Pate reports that solar lease rates run $750 to $1,400 per acre per year, depending on location and contract terms; even at the low end of that range, solar leasing generates more revenue per acre than barley, oats, or hay production.

Solar lease income: $750–$1,400/acre/year (varies by location and contract) often exceeds returns from lower-value crops like hay or oats (Carey & Pate, 2025)

Kunskaja & Budzyński (2026) found that lease payments to landowners represent one of the most consistent socioeconomic benefits of solar deployment; reliable, annual, and unaffected by weather, pest, or market fluctuations that make traditional crop income unpredictable. For many rural NC landowners, a solar lease is the first stable income their land has generated in years.

"At the local level, renewable energy projects stimulate economic development through higher household incomes, lease payments to landowners, and increased tax revenues for local governments"
- (Kunskaja & Budzyński, 2026.)

HB 729 would make NC a less attractive destination for new solar leases. Developers who face cost increases on existing projects will price that policy risk into future contracts, leading to lower lease offers, or no offers at all, for rural landowners who have not yet to make a deal.

Think about who that affects. The rural landowner in your district who has been watching their neighbor's lease check come in every January and has been waiting for their own opportunity. The farmer whose land isn't ideal for crops anymore but could host panels. The family that wants to stay on their land but needs supplemental income to do it. A young farmer who needs the guaranteed lease payments from a solar project to afford to buy new equipment or purchase more livestock for the agricultural portion of their farm. HB 729 doesn't protect them. It closes a door that was finally starting to open.

AGRIVOLTAICS

What the science says about solar and farmland co-existing.

“AVS provide co-benefits such as enhanced crop/pasture water-use efficiencies (up to 150–300 % improvement), greater land-use efficiency (up to 200 %), reduced irrigation demand (14 % reduction), improved profitability (up to 15 times higher revenue) and more consistent interannual crop/pasture production compared with conventional agricultural production systems in isolation”
- (Pandey et al., 2025)

Pandey et al. (2025) examined farms where solar panels and active agriculture share the same land and found that using the land for both solar and farming often works better than doing just one or the other

Key findings from the study:

-Up to 150–300% improvement in crop water-use efficiency under solar canopies
-14% reduction in irrigation demand, directly relevant to NC's rainfall-dependent agriculture
-Solar panels moderate soil temperature extremes, reducing crop stress
-Solar panel performance itself improved in agrivoltaic settings due to vegetative cooling
-Livestock grazing and pollinator habitats can coexist with active solar installations
-Profitability up to 15 times higher in agrivoltaic systems compared to conventional agriculture alone

The concern at the heart of HB 729 is that solar removes agricultural land from productive use. That concern is worth taking seriously. Pandey et al. (2025) found that in agrivoltaic systems, where solar and active agriculture share the same land, the combination often outperforms either use alone, particularly in hot or drought-prone conditions like much of NC's agricultural south. Rather than seeing the solar footprint on NC agricultural land as a loss, we should try to see it as a way to use the same land for multiple things; farm income, local tax revenue, growing food, etc.

Right now, solar covers less than 1% of North Carolina’s farmland. Instead of treating solar as a "loss" of land, we should see it as a "managed asset." If legislators are actually worried about protecting farms, they should encourage smarter solar rather than seeking to kill the tax incentives. By setting standards for agrivoltaics, like requiring space for sheep grazing or pollinator habitats under the panels, we can keep the land in production, keep the tax revenue flowing into the county, and keep the farming profitable for the next generation.

The Full Case

Evidence, costs, and alternatives

The strongest version of each argument, for and against HB 729, is examined below alongside the available evidence.

(Pandey et al., 2025)
"Agrivoltaic systems can provide a more efficient use of land resources… and lead to a more synergistic relationship between food and energy production."
- Pandey et al. (2025), p. 849

Pandey et al. (2025) conducted a review of the agrivoltaics literature, synthesizing the entire body of research on what happens when solar panels and agriculture actually share the same land. That matters here because HB 729’s central claim is that solar harms farmland. The research here says the opposite, and it says it consistently across dozens of independent studies.

The water-use efficiency finding is the most consequential one for NC specifically. North Carolina agriculture is heavily dependent on rainfall and irrigation. That improvement in water-use efficiency means the same crop requires dramatically less irrigation; solar panels reduce evaporation and moderate soil temperature, stabilizing moisture levels during NC's hot summer months. That is a direct, documented benefit to the farmer operating under them.

What this means is that if HB 729 sponsors genuinely want to protect farmland, they are restricting the wrong thing. The data says agrivoltaic systems make farmland more productive, not less. Eliminating the abatement that makes these installations financially viable removes the economic incentive for a land-use model that data says benefits agriculture. The land-use data makes the bill's title difficult to defend. Single-family housing occupies 7.18% of NC agricultural land. The bill is called the Farmland Protection Act, but the data does not support that name.

(Kunskaja & Budzyński, 2026)
"These benefits are, however, heterogeneous and depend on enabling conditions such as policy stability, financial development, grid integration, innovation capacity, and social acceptance. "
— Kunskaja & Budzyński (2026), Solar, 6(1), 3

Kunskaja & Budzyński (2026) conducted a review of studies on the societal welfare outcomes of solar and renewable energy deployment across multiple countries, using SALSA and PRISMA methodology. Their findings confirm that solar consistently produces economic welfare gains; employment growth, higher household incomes, increased local tax revenue, but with one critical condition: those gains depend on policy stability. It is the first enabling condition they identify. Without it, the documented benefits shrink.

This is the direct basis for why HB 729 is damaging beyond the projects already in the ground. NC built millions in estimated rural investment on the predictability of the 2008 abatement. Cutting the exclusion in half, with no transition period and no grandfathering, signals to every future developer evaluating NC that the state's policy commitments are subject to revision after capital has been committed. Kunskaja & Budzyński document what follows: developers reprice their exposure, future projects stall or relocate, and the communities that were still in line to receive the next wave of investment don't. The bill doesn't just harm what was built. It forecloses what could have been.

(Hernandez et al., 2019)
"The strategic engineering of solar energy technologies—from individual rooftop modules to large solar energy power plants—can confer significant synergistic outcomes across industrial and ecological boundaries."
— (Hernandez et al., 2019)

Hernandez et al. (2019) introduced the concept of techno-ecological synergy (TES), a framework for engineering mutually beneficial relationships between solar installations and its surrounding ecological systems. The study identified 16 distinct synergies between solar energy infrastructure and natural systems, spanning land, food, water, and built environments.

The relevance to HB 729 is quite direct. The bill's farmland protection premise assumes that solar installations and agricultural or ecological land use are in conflict, where one displaces the other. Hernandez et al. says the opposite: that thoughtfully sited and engineered solar installations can create measurable ecological benefits on the same land, including soil stabilization, pollinator habitat, reduced evaporation, and improved microclimate conditions for surrounding vegetation.

For NC legislators weighing HB 729, this matters in a specific way. The bill's prohibition on new utility-scale solar on agricultural land assumes the land-use tradeoff is zero-sum. The evidence here clearly says it is not. The question is not whether solar or farmland, it is whether the installations are designed with ecological co-benefits in mind. HB 729 forecloses that design question entirely by prohibiting the installations outright.

Real Counterarguments and the Response

Counterargument 1: Solar locks up farmland for 25–35 years. This is the bill's most legitimate concern. Solar lease terms are long, and a landowner who signs a lease is effectively removing it from traditional crop rotation for a generation. For agricultural communities worried about long-term food production capacity, that is a real concern worth taking seriously.

The response: the bill's answer, restricting solar on agricultural land and taxing installations, is far broader than the problem it claims to solve. A targeted response would require agrivoltaic practices for new approvals on prime agricultural land, not a ban. Pandey et al. (2025) documented that agrivoltaic systems maintain at least 80% of crop yield while generating solar energy, the science already provides the compromise, but HB 729 ignores it.

Counterargument 2: The 80% abatement is unfair to non-solar landowners who pay full tax rates. This is a genuine equity argument. A landowner who does not host solar panels pays property tax on 100% of assessed value, while their neighbor with panels pays on only 20%. That disparity is real and legislators from non-solar-heavy districts have legitimate standing to raise it.

The response: the solution is a prospective reform with adequate transition time, not taxation. If the General Assembly wants to reduce the exclusion for new installations going forward, that conversation is legitimate and can be had without punishing the developers and counties that built the current system in good faith. The application of HB 729 is what makes it indefensible, not the underlying policy question it is trying to address.

Alternatives Analysis

Alternative 1: Pass HB 729 as written. This cuts the exclusion from 80% to 0% and prohibits new utility-scale solar on most agricultural land. Counties would see immediate reductions in property tax revenue from existing solar installations, revenue already budgeted for schools and emergency services. Developers also face cost increases they never agreed to. This would signal to future investors, who want to invest in solar projects in NC, that its incentive structures are subject to revision, which would freeze the pipeline of future projects.

Alternative 2: Negotiate a modified abatement. A reduced abatement, say 60% rather than 80%, would increase county tax revenue from existing solar projects while preserving some investment incentive going forward. This is a theoretically viable middle path, but it still applies a cost increase to existing projects, maintains the breach of investment certainty, and is not currently on the legislative table.

The cleanest and most defensible compromise is a clean vote No on HB 729 as filed. It requires no new legislation, no negotiation, and no state spending. It simply keeps a working policy intact. If the legislature wants to revisit the abatement structure in a future session, that conversation can happen then. But that is a different bill.

A Final Word

Every number came from a study or a documented government record. The concerns behind HB 729: farmland preservation, tax equity, rural land use are legitimate concerns that deserve serious policy responses. This site has tried to take them seriously, and to show what the available evidence says about whether this specific bill addresses them well.

The legislature that created this opportunity in 2008 did not spend state money or mandate anything. They simply created conditions for private capital to flow into rural NC; and it did, at a scale that transformed county budgets across 50 counties. That is a legacy worth protecting.

The science on agrivoltaics shows that solar and farmland can coexist productively and that co-location requirements would address farmland concerns more precisely than an abatement cut. The fiscal data shows that rural NC counties have built real budget gains on the stability of the 2008 policy. Landowners who have not yet signed solar leases stand to benefit from a stable investment environment, not a chilled one.

A vote No on HB 729 as written isn't a vote against farmland protection, but for finding a way to address legitimate land-use concerns without dismantling the fiscal foundation that rural NC built over 17 years.
